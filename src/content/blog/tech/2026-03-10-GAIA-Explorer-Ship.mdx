## 🚀 1. Core Physical Requirements

For an object to fly through space and survive solar wind, dust, and radiation, it must satisfy
at least these scientific requirements:

- **Structural integrity** - must withstand vacuum, temperature swings, and mechanical loads from
  maneuvers and impacts.
- **Thermal control** - must manage heat purely by radiation and limited conduction, since there
  is no air for convection.
- **Radiation tolerance** - materials, electronics, and (if present) crew must tolerate ionizing
  radiation from the Sun and cosmic rays.
- **Micrometeoroid and dust protection** - must survive high-velocity impacts from dust and small
  particles.
- **Plasma and charging control** - must handle interaction with solar wind plasma and avoid
  dangerous charge buildup.
- **Attitude and trajectory control** - must be able to orient and steer itself in a near-frictionless
  environment.

## 🌬️ 2. Solar Wind and Plasma Environment

Solar wind is a continuous stream of charged particles (mostly protons and electrons) emitted by
the Sun. It imposes unique engineering demands on every exposed surface.

### Requirements

- **Conductive exterior or controlled grounding**
  To prevent differential charging, the outer structure uses conductive materials or controlled
  grounding paths so charge does not build up and arc across components.

- **Surface material selection**
  Materials must tolerate:
  - Sputtering (atoms knocked off by particle impacts)
  - UV degradation
  - Erosion of coatings and optics

- **EMI and EMC design**
  Electronics must be shielded and filtered against:
  - Fluctuating magnetic fields
  - Particle-induced noise and single-event effects

- **Solar sail potential** _(optional realism layer)_
  Large, thin reflective surfaces can generate thrust from photons and, to a lesser extent,
  charged particles. Control requires precise attitude control and very low areal density.

## ☄️ 3. Cosmic Dust and Micrometeoroids

Space is filled with micrometeoroids and dust traveling at tens of kilometers per second.
Even tiny grains carry enormous kinetic energy.

### Requirements

- **Impact shielding**
  Common engineering approaches:
  - _Whipple shield_ - a thin outer bumper that shatters the particle before it hits the main wall.
  - _Multi-layer shield_ - several spaced layers that progressively disperse and vaporize the impactor.

- **Redundant critical systems**
  Because a single impact can destroy a component, critical systems (power, data, life support)
  are:
  - Fully redundant
  - Physically separated across the hull

- **Penetration-tolerant structural design**
  Pressure vessels, tanks, and crew modules are designed so that:
  - Small punctures do not cause catastrophic failure
  - Leaks can be isolated and repaired before cascading

## ☢️ 4. Radiation Environment

Radiation in deep space comes from multiple sources:

- Solar particle events (protons and heavier ions from flares and coronal mass ejections)
- Galactic cosmic rays (high-energy particles from outside the solar system)
- Trapped radiation belts near planets

### Requirements

- **Electronics shielding**
  - Use aluminum or composite structures to reduce accumulated dose.
  - Harden electronics to withstand:
    - Total ionizing dose
    - Single-event upsets and latchups

- **Crew shielding** _(if crewed)_
  - Place mass around crew areas using:
    - Water tanks
    - Food stores
    - Dedicated radiation storm shelters
  - Plan missions to:
    - Limit time in high-radiation regions
    - Provide a shelter protocol for solar particle events

- **Radiation-tolerant design patterns**
  - Error-correcting memory
  - Watchdog timers and autonomous reset strategies
  - Redundant computers with voting logic

## 🌡️ 5. Thermal Control in Vacuum

In space, a vehicle gains heat from three sources:

- Direct sunlight
- Planetary infrared emission
- Internal power dissipation

It loses heat **almost entirely by thermal radiation** - there is no atmosphere to carry it away.

### Requirements

- **Radiators and insulation**
  - Radiator panels sized to reject the maximum expected waste heat load.
  - Multi-layer insulation (MLI) blankets to reduce unwanted heat gain or loss during transit.

- **Surface optical properties**
  - Coatings selected for specific absorptivity and emissivity ratios per surface zone.

- **Active thermal control**
  - Heaters to keep cold-sensitive components within operating range.
  - Fluid loops or heat pipes to transport heat from sources to radiators.

## 🧭 6. Attitude, Propulsion, and Control

To traverse real space the ship must control both its orientation and its trajectory with precision.

### Propulsion

- Chemical, electric, nuclear, or hybrid propulsion system.
- Sufficient delta-v budget for:
  - Routine course corrections
  - Orbital insertion and departure burns
  - Emergency hazard avoidance

### Attitude Control

- Reaction wheels, control moment gyros, or thrusters for orientation.
- Sensor suite:
  - Star trackers
  - Sun sensors
  - Inertial measurement units (IMUs)

### Guidance and Navigation

Onboard algorithms must:

- Predict trajectories under gravity and small perturbations.
- Schedule burns and maneuvers autonomously.
- Account for communication delays and limited ground-contact windows.

## 🎮 7. Putting It Together for GAIA Explorer

To make the GAIA Explorer ship feel scientifically grounded in gameplay:

### Give the ship these visible properties

- A conductive hull with visible radiator panels
- Whipple-style shielding layers or explicit micrometeoroid armor plating
- A hardened radiation shelter or heavily shielded crew core
- Radiators that visually glow or shift color under thermal load
- Physically separated, redundant module layout

### Make the hazards behave realistically

| Hazard | Gameplay effect |
| -- | -- |
| Solar storm | Radiation spike, increased sensor noise, comm blackout |
| Dust-rich region | Rising impact risk, sensor and radiator degradation |
| Long deep-space transit | Cumulative radiation damage, crew stress, system wear |
| Near massive star | Thermal overload, hull stress, navigation difficulty |

> The more the ship's systems mirror real spacecraft engineering, the more meaningful the
> resource management and hazard avoidance decisions become for the player.
